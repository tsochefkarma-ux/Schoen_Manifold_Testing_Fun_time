## Where we began

At leading order, we found an exact square-lattice curvature closure at (\tau=i). The important part was not only that the identity held, but that we identified its mechanism.

The cleared leading defect satisfies

[
N_1\in\langle LE_2-6,;E_6\rangle.
]

Thus the closure follows from the two modular fixed-point conditions

[
LE_2(i)=6,
\qquad
E_6(i)=0.
]

We then established:

[
\det J
======

29859840,C_0^4L^7(L^2E_4+36)^2\neq0,
]

so the closure is locally rigid in the two transverse modular directions.

We also found that the identity is transverse along the physical modular path (\tau=iy): its first variation has no positive radial zero. In other words, the closure is not a generic modular identity; it is genuinely pinned to the square-lattice point.

The arbitrary six-jet linearisation gave a rank-two obstruction map with a four-dimensional preserving kernel. Two kernel directions were familiar—rescaling and the formal (E_4) tangent—while two further formal preserving directions were nontrivial.

Those are the strongest **exact symbolic results** from the first paper.

That became the degree-two obstruction paper.

# What we have not proved yet

The distinction remains important.

## Exact or symbolic

The leading fixed-point closure, ideal membership, rigidity calculation, transversality polynomial, and finite-jet rank results are exact or rigorously certified.

## High-precision evidence

The degree-two spectral connection is currently a very strongly validated **numerical rational-function phenomenon**:

* 1280-bit arithmetic;
* up to 70 radial points;
* numerous held-out points;
* arbitrary rational geometries;
* rank stability from (10^{-30}) to (10^{-345});
* no row saturation;
* residuals hundreds of decimal places below the working scale.

But we have not yet derived the full connection coefficients symbolically.

We therefore should currently say:

> High-precision computation strongly supports a unique minimal (U)-Laurent spectral connection at degree two.

We should not yet call it an exact theorem.
